About Me

I’m a researcher in embedded and energy-harvesting systems, with a focus on ubiquitous computing. My work involves designing energy and latency-adaptive hardware-software co-design systems integrated with cross-stack optimizations to achive the best energy-efficiency. By developing hardware-software mechanism to achieve timely computation and communication in batteryless sensor nodes, my goal is to push the boundaries of what’s possible in these tiny, resilient sensor node technology for various environments, including agriculture, infrastructure, and even outer space.

Prior to my Ph.D, I completed my bachelors from SCMS School of Engineering and Technology, Cochin, Kerala, India. Outside research, I enjoy, travel adventures, working out in gym, cooking, playing volleyball, podcasts and reading, watching Manchester United and manga.

BOBBER: A Prototyping platform for batteryless intermittent accelerators

Batteryless intermittent architectures are optimized for energy-efficiency rather than operating power or performance alone, due to performance of these systems dictated solely by energy harvesting and energy-efficiency. Real world applications (like crop health monitoring, wildfire monitoring etc.,) require nodes to perform energy-efficient operation for certain computations (like Neural network inference), without having the need to rely on centralized processing (like cloud). To ensure this, a node should have energy and latency-adaptive heterogeneous specialized cores. With this goal, we developed BOBBER, an FPGA based prototyping platform for batteryless intermittent accelerators. BOBBER is a full stack solution with an MSP430 microcontroller that runs application software on top of an intermittency-aware firmware. BOBBER enables common neural network optimizations like reduced precision, sparsity, that current state-of-the-art batteryless intermittent accelerators lack, hence enabling timely computing with support for both hardware and software adaptivity.

A HW-SW mechanism to enable timely communication and sensing in batteryless intermittently-powered sensor networks -- (work in progress)

Batteryless operation has enabled applications where ultra-low cost, independence from infrastructure, and spatial sensor resolution are more important than bandwidth. These applications demand sampling rates ranging from 1 sample per 5 s to 1 sample per 200s. Although these applications have been made feasible, current batteryless sensor node approaches still rely on direct connections to high-cost, continuously powered gateway nodes (e.g., battery-powered cellular-enabled nodes, wired RFID readers) due to their inability to operate in a timely fashion, i.e., in a coordinated manner. Such architecture becomes limited by the cost and infrastructure reliance of the continuously powered nodes. In general nodes must guarantee to wake up at the same time to sense and communicate to another node, before the sensed date becomes stale. By enabling coordination between batteryless nodes, without having to depend on an always-ON node, the cost of pervasive deployment can be significantly reduced and made more resilient. For the purpose of robust coordination, we present the first hardware-based approach that enables predictable and adaptive intermittent wakeups despite variable and low energy harvesting rates. Our approach uses a hardware component that enforces a constant amount of time between node wakeups, with small variations. The software component dynamically corrects for those variations. Our approach guarantees that a node wakes up at the same time with guaranteed energy ensuring robust coordination of on-times for batteryless intermittently-powered systems.

Heterogenous and adaptive intermittent lifecycling using heterogeneous microarchitectures-- (work in progress)

Batteryless intermittently-powered sensor nodes are deployed with no intelligence of the energy harvesting environment (or context) -- an environment which in reality can show heterogenous behavior (both in magnitude and variation) for the harvested rate. Given the different energy harvesting contexts where batteryless intermittently-powered sensor nodes are deployed, traditionally a node is either designed for a worst-case like scenario or for a best-case like scenario. Such nodes incur wastage of latent energy within the system and are not guaranteed to be available for timeliness needs for communication or sensing. Rather a node must be able operate at a worst-case like scenario, a best-case scenario and use the latent energy within the system to ensure energy-efficient computation for all contexts in between.
